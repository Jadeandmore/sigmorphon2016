Welcome

The SIGMORPHON 2016 shared task is morphological paradigm completion. The goal, roughly stated, is to fill out a morphological paradigm, a nominal or verbal inflection table, given some incomplete representation of the whole paradigm, e.g., the lemma or a partial (potentially noisy) set of slots of the paradigm. This area of computational morphology has received a flurry of attention recently in the NLP literature (Dreyer and Eisner (2009), Dreyer and Eisner (2011), Durett an DeNero (2013), Hulden et al. (2014), Nicolai and Kondrak (2015), inter alia).

Paradigm Completion – A Brief Introduction

In many of the world's languages words change their form to express different syntactic and semantic relationships in a structured fashion. For instance, English generally overtly marks the plural with a final "s", as in "model" → "models". Even for novel words, native speakers are perfectly capable of generating the correct inflections (consider "wug" → "wugs"), making these processes highly productive. The act of modifying word forms to express these relationships is termed inflection (often conjugation when referring to verb and declension when referring to nouns). In English, this task is relatively uninteresting, e.g., English substantives only distinguish singular and plural. But many of the world's languages exhibit a high degree of inflection making this a hard computational problem. In Polish, for example, a single verb can have over a 100 forms! This sort of linguistic productivity creates data sparsity problems. With this shared task, we hope to make a dent in our ability to automate this aspect of computational morphology.

The Languages

In our selection of languages, we have sought a representative sample of the world's languages (albeit biased to those with rich inflection). All of the data has been scraped from Wiktionary and undergone additional processing at the Center for Language and Speech Processing as Johns Hopkins University and formatted according to schema described in Sylak-Glassman et al. (2015).
